About the California Current Integrated Ecosystem Assessment

The California Current Integrated Ecosystem Assesment (CCIEA) is an interdisciplinary research effort led by NOAA scientists along the US West Coast. Our goal is to provide science support for ecosystem-based management of the California Current—a complex ecosystem in which natural and human systems are inextricably linked.

What is Ecosystem Based Management?

Ecosystem based management (EBM) is a holistic approach to environmental management that considers the entire array of interactions within an ecosystem, including humans. Rather than focusing on single species, issues, or sectors in isolation, this approach considers multiple interacting ecosystem components (for example, zooplankton, sardines, sardine predators, and sardine fisheries), as well as the cumulative impacts of numerous human activities and the protection of ecosystem structure, function, and key processes. In the marine realm, EBM has been endorsed by management organizations at all levels worldwide and has become a central paradigm underlying NOAA’s living marine resource policy in the United States.

For more information, go to national EBM page.

What is IEA and how can it support Ecosystem Based Management?

Integrated ecosystem assessment (IEA) is a framework used for organizing the science needed to inform EBM decisions. The initial IEA framework (Levin et al. 2009) outlines key steps that enhance the likelihood of successful EBM implementation. Some of these steps include:

  • defining specific ecosystem objectives;
  • assessing ecosystem status through development and monitoring of indicators;
  • identifying and prioritizing key sources of risk; and
  • quantitative ecosystem modeling to evaluate the potential of different management strategies to mitigate risk and achieve objectives.

IEA is both an incremental approach to decision theory and an iterative process, where scientific understanding feeds an array of management tradeoffs, and balances feedback from changing ecosystem objectives. Furthermore, the IEA framework can be scaled to yield applications and products that meet the complexity or geography of specific EBM objectives (Harvey et al. 2017).

For more information, go to national IEA home page: https://www.integratedecosystemassessment.noaa.gov/index.html .

How do we conceptualize the California Current ecosystem in a way that helps us study and monitor it?

To better support the interdisciplinary dialogue necessary for a successful IEA process, we conceptualize the California Current as a social-ecological system which explicitly acknowledges linkages and feedbacks between human and biophysical systems at multiple scales (Levin et al. 2016).

The biophysical environment (Figure 1, left) is organized into three major tiers: climate and ocean drivers, habitat, and focal components of ecosystem integrity. Key climate and ocean drivers for the California Current include large scale atmospheric conditions (e.g., ENSO, PDO) that affect ocean circulation and temperature, and regional drivers such as upwelling strength and timing. In the middle tier, habitat availability and condition mediate these drivers and provide the matrix through which ecosystem interactions occur. At the top are focal components or living resources, including species like marine mammals, salmon, and sardines that are foundationally important and highly valued members of the of the California Current ecosystem.

The human dimension (Figure 1, right) of the California Current is similarly organized by tier, and is composed of social drivers, local social systems, and human well-being. Large-scale social drivers, such as global economic systems, population growth and settlement patterns, and dominant cultural values, are external forces that constrain or enable local social systems and human activities. Local social systems such as state or local laws, regional economies, and social networks affect human wellbeing directly or indirectly and moderate how human activities interact with the natural environment. Human well-being can be measured in a number of ways, but essentially represents a state where human needs are met, individuals can enjoy a satisfactory quality of life, and people can act meaningfully to pursue their goals.

At the center of Figure 1 are human activities, the most tangible points of connection between the social and ecological systems, and the focus of most management actions. In the California Current, ocean-based activities include fishing, recreational pursuits, transportation of goods and services, energy extraction, and the construction and maintenance of associated systems. A range of land-based activities similarly interact with coastal environments, including infrastructure (e.g., dams, ports) or activities that affect the timing and magnitude of freshwater and sediment transport, nutrient or contaminant inputs, farming and aquaculture operations, and public access and tourism.
