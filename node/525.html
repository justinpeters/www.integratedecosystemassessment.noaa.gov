Mid-Barataria Sediment Diversions

Gulf of Mexico Project

 

Gulf of Mexico Integrated Ecosystem Assessment provides objective science for management

NOAA’s Gulf of Mexico Integrated Ecosystem Assessment (GoM IEA) team is using the Integrated Ecosystem Assessment approach to provide the State of Louisiana and others ecosystem science important to effectively manage the loss of coastal wetlands, sustain valuable marine resources, and sustain the needs of coastal communities. An important part of the Integrated Ecosystem Assessment (IEA) approach is to detect and predict trade-offs between different management scenarios.

The State of Louisiana is proposing the construction of a number of sediment diversions of Mississippi River water and sediment to stem the catastrophic loss of coastal wetlands in Louisiana. This provided an excellent opportunity for the NOAA IEA program to pilot implementation of the IEA approach to detect and predict trade-offs for this management scenario. The IEA approach supports an adaptive management framework for the Mississippi River sediment diversions. Information from the Integrated Ecosystem Assessment of the Mid-Barataria Sediment Diversion Project will integrate into the decision making throughout operations of the project.

There are many agencies and organizations involved in this decision-making process. The Louisiana Coastal Protection and Restoration Authority (CPRA) has applied for a permit for the Mid-Barataria sediment diversion (MBSD) near Myrtle Grove, Louisiana. The U.S. Army Corp of Engineers is the permitting agency. The sediment diversion would be a permanent structure built within the levee structure/system. All proposed diversions will include a robust adaptive management plan to manage diversion operations to optimize intended outcomes and minimize deleterious impacts to the ecosystem.

An Integrated Ecosystem Assessment of the Mid-Barataria Basin:

As part of the proposal to divert sediment from the Mississippi River into the Baratarian Basin, the GoM IEA program is carrying out an Integrated Ecosystem Assessment on the Barataria Basin in coastal Louisiana in order to help establish baseline conditions.

The first step of the Integrated Ecosystem Assessment for this project was gathering scientists, managers, and stakeholders together to define the broad vision and objectives of the project. The goal of the sediment diversion project is to maximize sediment transport from the Mississippi River to the Barataria Basin to build, sustain, and maintain land.

In February 2018 the GoM IEA team held a workshop with many stakeholders from federal and state agencies, academia, NGOs, and others involved in the diversions project to identify what indicators are relevant to measure and predict impacts of the diversion.

This team of scientific experts then identified a preliminary suite of ecological indicators to assess
the status, natural variability, and long-term trends of the Barataria Basin prior to the diversion construction and operation to meet wetland restoration goals.

Data for these indicators are gathered from a multitude of scientific resources. Identifying these indicators and assessing them establish a stronger understanding of societies relationship to the ecosystem. Part of identifying these indicators included assessing if the data currently being collected within the Barataria Basin is sufficient to inform managers and the adaptive management process. This will facilitate an assessment of both the state of the ecosystem and human communities along the coast and help determine if the diversions are being optimally planned and operated.

Impacts of the diversion to humans and their well-being

The Integrated Ecosystem Assessment approach recognizes the importance of understanding the connections between humans and ecosystems, and the significant role that the environment plays in human well-being and incorporates that into the decision-making process. So the GoM IEA program is working to include impacts of the diversion to humans and their well-being.

For ecologists and coastal managers the Barataria Basin is a wetland but for its residents, this place evokes strong emotional, historic and economic associations. Agencies working on this project are aware of the importance of discussing societal goals for this project and the Coastal Protection and Restoration Authority (CPRA) planning document emphasizes the fact that this project has been created with the local communities in mind. So there have been efforts to gain an understanding of the public opinion.

The GoM IEA team is identifying the best method to include a broad and inclusive public opinion on this project. A literature review was done of different methods of engaging local communities in management decisions. A possible solution would be to figure out through ethnographic research how to best represent the various communities in the Basin, develop relations and trust with key representants and then design a long-term program that allows these communities to come up with some of their own solutions to this problem.

In addition to ethnographic research, the GoM IEA program also is in the process of developing consensus around a results chain to include the impact to humans and thoughts of all things important to local communities into the decision-making process.

The results chain is as follows below:

  1. If we deliver sediment, freshwater, and nutrients then we reduce land loss 
  2. If we reduce land loss then we sustain the Deepwater Horizon injured wetlands
  3. If we sustain wetlands then we reduce long-term impacts from sea-level rise and storms
  4. If we reduce long-term impacts from sea-level rise and storms then we protect historic properties and traditional living cultures and their ties and relationships to the natural environment
  5. If we protect historic properties and traditional living cultures then we sustain the unique cultural heritage of Coastal Louisiana

Informing the adaptive management process

The GoM IEA is using these indicators, qualitative modeling methods, such as results chains and Bayesian belief networks to elicit and analyze expert opinion and inform the adaptive management process.

Ultimately the GoM IEA approach will provide information to managers for decision-making processes (regulatory commenting and consultation) associated with diversion operations and inform the development of an adaptive management process to monitor post diversion.
